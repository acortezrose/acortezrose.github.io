March 10, 2020

Recently, I’ve been listening to ambient music from morning to night. For a few moments, instead of sitting at home under a harsh reading light, infiltrated with the distant clanging of pots and pans, I can be somewhere else entirely—standing in an endless haze of tall grass, listening to a synthesized swell of soft rain.

I was playing Wet Land by Hiroshi Yoshimura as I read assignment descriptions for my privacy class. One titled Privacy Art simply stated, “Produce a piece of privacy-related art and write a reflection on your piece.”

I thought about the different records I’d been listening to and how they portrayed information through sound. Michael Siegel’s The Sounds of the Office creates an archive of office sounds in their ordinary environment: coffee breaks, letter opening, typing. While seemingly mundane, this soundscape paints a precise picture of the ecosystem of a single office as it existed in 1964—complete with dictating machines and typewriters. NASA’s Symphonies of the Planets drones and swirls on, endlessly re-imagining our solar system's planets through Voyager data. If these ideas could be manifested as music, then privacy should be able to as well, I thought.
